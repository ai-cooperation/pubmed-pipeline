## Abstract

The Stroke Treatment Academic Industry Roundtable convened a workshop regarding artificial intelligence (AI) and innovative clinical trial designs during the Stroke Treatment Academic Industry Roundtable XIII meeting on March 28, 2025. This forum brought together stroke physicians and researchers, and industry representatives to discuss the current use and future opportunities for AI and novel trial designs in acute stroke trials. AI already plays a substantial role in the treatment of acute stroke with regards to imaging but is poised to have a much larger impact in clinical care and research trials over the coming years. The quality and understanding of the data are used to train the AI, the human element needed to ensure training is successful, and the clinician and trialist at the bedside, the humans "in the loop," will be necessary to maximize AI's effectiveness in clinical practice and trials. Platform trials address multiple scientific questions in an area of medicine simultaneously within the same trial structure by sharing controls across multiple interventions. While platform trials increase efficiency and potentially decrease the time needed to answer important clinical scientific questions, they also can introduce complexity to standard workflows. Future acute ischemic stroke clinical trials should incorporate elements of pragmatic and patient-centered trial design when possible. Pragmatic trials aim to assess the effectiveness of treatments when they are implemented into routine clinical care rather than under idealized conditions. AI models and platform, pragmatic, and patient-centered trial designs are new tools to answer important clinical questions, but understanding how they work, their best uses, and their limitations is critical for accelerating successful new treatments for stroke.

### Introduction:

The Stroke Treatment Academic Industry Roundtable (STAIR) convened a session and workshop regarding Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Novel Trial Designs for Acute Ischemic Stroke during the STAIR XIII meeting on March 28, 2025. This forum brought together stroke physicians and researchers and industry representatives to discuss the opportunities and challenges of the rapidly evolving technology of artificial intelligence (AI) and the new trial design of platforms for acute stroke. Like all tools, we need to understand how these new tools work, their best use, and their limitations.

### Artificial Intelligence in Acute Stroke Practice and Trials (

Artificial intelligence (AI) is becoming a valuable tool for supporting timely and accurate medical decision-making. Its ability to rapidly analyze large volumes of complex patient data can offer critical insights, particularly in time-sensitive conditions like stroke, where minutes count. AI-assisted image interpretation has been shown to be particularly beneficial in settings where immediate access to expert radiologists is limited, such as community or rural stroke centers. Rapid expert radiology readings are also limited in urban centers. These tools help ensure systematic analysis regardless of the availability of local expertise. By integrating AI into diagnostic workflows, clinicians can enhance their assessments and increase their ability to interpret more scans per shift with data-driven support.

It is helpful to frame any discussion regarding the use of AI in healthcare with six principles outlined by the World Health Organization:1 1) protect autonomy. 2) promote human well-being and safety and protect the public interest, 3) ensure transparency, explainability, and intelligibility, 4) foster responsibility and accountability, 5) ensure inclusiveness and equity, 6) promote AI that is responsive and sustainable. The key here is the importance of having “humans-in-the-loop” at every step: planning, explaining, including, calibrating, and measuring positive and negative impacts. In other words, how do we turn the black box of AI inside out so everyone can grasp what is happening and can provide input, not just the original designers.

Broadly speaking, AI ca be classified into three distinct process domains: robotic process automation (RPA), machine learning (ML) and generative AI (Gen AI). RPA is used to automate a set of rule-based repetitive steps, such as using a source document to fill out and submit a claim form. ML leverages various mathematical models (e.g., convolutional neural networks, regression, decision trees) to predict the likelihood of a future event or classify an outcome or finding as being present. Each model is typically trained for one specific task. Gen AI is built on complex “transformer” architecture, enabling advanced processing of sequential data like language. This allows so called “large language models” to ingest massive amounts of unstructured data to capture deeper contextual relationships and produce novel human like outputs like text, pictures, sounds and videos.

AI already plays a critical role in time-sensitive scenarios like the standard-of-care treatment of acute stroke. AI image analysis software applications (e.g., RAPID, Viz.ai and Brainomix) provide real-time assessment of brain and blood vessel imaging including estimates of the volume of brain that has a high likelihood of infarction (core), the volume of brain that has decreased perfusion but may be salvageable (penumbra), the presence of a large vessel occlusion, and the presence of hemorrhage in the brain or surrounding the brain.2 Although not currently in use clinically, validated deep learning models using a standard 12 lead ECG can estimate the presence of structural heart disease or the future risk of atrial fibrillation in the evaluation of a stroke patient without current evidence of atrial fibrillation.3 A range of AI-based predictive models are being developed, including wearables, smartphone apps, smart home sensors, and novel diagnostic tools with the goal of facilitating timely detection of stroke symptoms. Depending on the specific application, these tools can promote symptom reporting, alert health care services, or assist EMS providers during prehospital triage.4

These software programs have achieved good or improving diagnostic accuracy for these conditions. Providing early notification that these conditions may be present can help clinicians and clinical trialists make real-time informed decisions at the bedside. With many of the current commercially available AI image analysis programs, imaging information is available to clinicians on their mobile phones or remote computers almost immediately after imaging is acquired. Thus, AI’s greatest usefulness currently is in rapidly alerting and focusing the expert clinician who confirms or rejects the AI interpretation, and decides if, and which type of reperfusion therapy may be appropriate.

Clinical trial enrollment can be enhanced by Gen AI’s ability to screen and identify potentially eligible patients based on medical images and clinical characteristics that can be accessed from the electronic medical record. Mobile apps can leverage these outputs to notify and activate the relevant research team members of potential candidates to screen for eligibility. This could be particularly useful for sites that are enrolling subjects for multiple competing clinical trials, where Gen AI algorithms could simultaneously assess eligibility for all trials and rank order the probability of eligibility, with subsequent mobile alerts to the research team members enabling swift action when multiple potentially eligible patients are identified. This approach can also streamline trial enrollment workflows by utilizing digital signatures for electronic consent (E-consent) and enhancing team-based communication regarding when consent and enrollment have occurred.5 AI can also be used to sift through and compile enrollment and performance data from all trial sites in real time, providing study sponsors with actionable analytics at the site, regional and national level.

### How to Train Your AI

The nature of data required in training the different types of AI models will vary by model. RPA models do not need to train on data but rather are programmed directly based on a derived set of rules. ML models are trained on structured and labeled data elements specific to the task of identifying patterns in existing data to classify or predict an outcome of interest which is known as the “ground truth”. Training often requires significant resources, especially related to applying annotations or labeling of critical variables as well as the ground truth target variable. The model’s predictions are compared against this ground truth to measure its performance. Unlike Gen AI models, most ML models are computationally efficient and can run on standard computing hardware. The more diverse and robust the training data, the more generalizable will be the model’s performance across sites of care. The training of ML AI models to identify the presence of large vessel occlusions, aneurysms, or brain hemorrhages in brain and vessel imaging has required the assemblage of large imaging data sets where the presence of these findings have been annotated as “ground truth” outcomes by human experts.2. A major advantage of these ML models is that their methods are generally more interpretable and their decision-making process more transparent, so they can be understood and traced, which is critical for medical validation and biological plausibility.

By contrast, Gen AI large language models (LLMs) are trained on massive, unlabeled bodies of text from the internet, books, and articles. They are then fine-tuned on smaller, specialized datasets and use these language models’ “understanding” to create new text, summarize documents, answer questions, and perform complex reasoning tasks. Training advanced models is extremely expensive and requires massive computing power and electricity to run dedicated graphical processing units (GPUs). The models themselves have billions or trillions of parameters, but they operate as a “black box,” making it difficult to fully understand how or why a specific output was generated. Explainability of LLMs is an active area of research.

AI models are only as good as the data used to train them. For example, if the data used to train an ML AI imaging program are restricted to a limited set of scanner manufacturers, or acquisition parameters such as slice thicknesses, model performance may be diminished on data generated from different imaging devices or using different parameters. As with imaging data, faulty or inaccurate data in the electronic health record (EHR) such as inaccurate medication lists, incorrect historical data, or inaccurately labeled variables can degrade model performance. The population of patients used to train the program may also affect its generalizability, and performance may be worse if the algorithm is applied to other populations that are substantially different from the training population. Datasets used for training and validation should include different age groups, races, ethnic groups, and social classes, whenever it is feasible to improve generalizability and mitigate potential biases. More research is needed to determine which structured (e.g., lab values, time stamps, vital signs) and unstructured (clinical notes, portal messages) data from the EHR can best be used to train ML AI algorithms with the least amount of effort. Different ML AI programs will focus on different source data elements, whereas Gen AI models will likely endeavor to consume as much structured and unstructured data as possible to build and train foundation models of medical reasoning and outcomes.

As with any new medical device, the AI model’s performance should be assessed pre-deployment by its developer against a set of standard, industry-endorsed set of benchmarks and validated on an external data set. Prior to deployment in a healthcare setting, the model should be locally validated in that healthcare setting to ensure it is performing as expected in this unique workflow and population environment. Lastly, healthcare systems should continue to monitor performance periodically to ensure there has been no deterioration in model performance over time, as the model may evolve or the workflows and patient populations change. For example, ML AI imaging algorithms should be trained on large volumes of data from a variety of imaging devices and protocols so they can be expected to perform successfully across a wide array of imaging devices. Validating models across populations and geographic areas additionally strengthens generalizability. In addition, ongoing monitoring of the AI program after implementation must occur as more data is used to improve the model, but also to determine if the AI program continues to perform as well over time. Unlike traditional static predictive models, both ML and GenAI models can be constructed to continue to refine their predictions and performance over time by ongoing learning methods (e.g., continuously training against the radiologist’s final interpretation and tracking disagreements between model and human outputs). This potential for model drift or planned model updates creates a regulatory challenge, and FDA regulations for software as a medical device (SaMD) have struggled to adequately address this potential for rapid or frequent model changes. On one hand, AI/ML-developed models can remain static after clinical deployment, but this approach undermines their potential performance and begs for a nimbler regulatory process to facilitate oversight of model evolution.

Like any tool, human interaction and expert oversight is critical for the safe, responsible and ethical use of AI. For example, perfusion imaging is highly dependent upon the quality of the raw imaging data and whether patient movement has resulted in poor data quality. The perfusion images must also be interpreted using the baseline non-contrast imaging of the brain to clarify if chronic lesions are present.6,7 That is why when physicians interpret CT perfusion images, they should always review the quality of the source images as well as the baseline non-contrast CT of the brain.6 This humans-in-the-loop strategy for clinical assessments and for clinical trials is also important to the FDA8 in their assessment of these diagnostic imaging tools.

### Current and Future Use of AI

At present, AI’s usefulness in clinical practice and clinical trials has been limited until recently mostly to pixel-based imaging analysis and predictions. This is in large part due to the simplicity and uniformity of imaging data making it suitable for ML AI algorithm training. However, AI has substantial potential to combine imaging and clinical data as well as the results of prior relevant clinical trials and scientific guidelines to provide clinical decision support to predict health outcomes, guide individualized treatment decisions, and match patients to available clinical trials. Part of the current challenge to deliver these capabilities is the linkage and ingestion of large amounts of imaging and clinical data for training AI models. In general, hospital systems appropriately limit the export of patient-level clinical data due to known challenges with achieving complete data deidentification to remove all protected health information (PHI) and personally Identifiable Information (PII) in conformance with HIPAA and state-specific privacy regulations. While many types of imaging data are easily deidentified, coronal reconstructions of cranial imaging can be used to generate facial maps that allow patient identification, and surface skin markings such as tattoos or birth marks may also reveal identity.9 The combination of clinical and imaging data could inadvertently expose PHI or PII and lead to a privacy breach, which would be amplified by the usual volume of patient records used to train these models. Protection of patient privacy represents a major challenge to the use of clinical data for training AI in healthcare, and sharing of even de-identified data between countries is made more challenging by different laws regarding data sharing in various countries. New methods of model development hold promise to address some of these privacy concerns. Federated learning models are machine learning models that are trained across multiple decentralized sites.10 The process involves distributing an initial model, which client sites update using their local raw data, sharing back centrally only local model weights or parameters without any raw data, and these updates are aggregated by the central server to create an improved global model. Another challenge that has not yet been definitively addressed is the nature of these predictive or generative outputs and if they should be included as part of the legal medical record or considered advisory to the clinician and discarded after use.

The workshop participants considered multiple solutions for secure, privacy-preserving linkage of various data sets from disparate sources and separate health systems to train AI models. Honest brokers independent of the AI research team, such as the American Heart Association Precision Medicine Platform and Get with The Guidelines™ initiatives, can be employed to centrally collect deidentified data and control access to authorized users. As noted previously, participants discussed the federated learning approach where AI models are trained locally on each institution’s or other data owner’s secure, private data, and only the model parameters are then shared for aggregation into a consensus AI model.10 This approach avoids the need for central collection of deidentified data and reduces the risk of inadvertent sharing of sensitive health information.

AI programs that incorporate multiple data sets, including prior relevant clinical trial data, could be used to model proposed clinical trials “in silico” to determine feasibility, ideal trial design approaches, and potential outcomes. In addition, randomized trials provide information about the overall average benefit and safety of treatment across selected populations. However, within a population, treatment effects can vary considerably. Knowing the treatment effect on an individual patient becomes difficult when a patient fits into multiple subgroups.11 AI ML models can be used to overcome this limitation to better estimate treatment effects in individual patients using increasing availability of rich historical datasets from previous clinical trials and real‐world data sources. One can leverage AI models to create holistic forecasts of future health outcomes for an individual patient in the form of an AI‐generated “digital twin.”12 This could eventually be implemented in clinical practice to make personalized medicine a reality.13

Another approach that incorporates multiple and/or large federated datasets is “target trial emulation”. 14,15 This is potentially suitable for answering pragmatic questions that are not testing new drug entities, or when a randomized trial is not feasible. Examples include blood pressure thresholds and timing of initiation of antiplatelet therapy for secondary prevention.

Currently, data from Get with the Guidelines and the population-based data from Greater Cincinnati and Northern Kentucky are used to evaluate the feasibility of proposed stroke trials within the NIH StrokeNet.16 But AI using larger and more diverse datasets could provide an even stronger tool to assess proposed trials. Such an AI program could also be used to estimate the potential number of potentially eligible patients at participating trial sites, as well as engaging specific patients or providers for enrollment, as the patients are evaluated in real time. Epic Systems is leveraging its massive clinical data warehouse to build out such GEN AI models that can perform these tasks. This could be particularly helpful at outlying hospitals that could enroll patients prior to transfer to a comprehensive academic stroke center. Cytoprotection trials, in which “dwell time” of the agent is of importance, could greatly benefit from these decentralized trial methods. Currently, AI’s role in clinical trials is limited to identifying relevant imaging findings. However, efforts to use AI in improving trial screening processes have shown promise in other fields; use of an AI-assisted screening tool resulted in higher enrollment in a cardiology implementation trial.17

Assessment of AI tool performance is required in cases where the AI tool is intended to perform tasks that inform human diagnostic or therapeutic decisions, for instance, the analysis of CT scans. Validation in independent external datasets not involved in training is required to assess performance. AI tool assistance in patient recruitment may be assessed retrospectively but should be based on fixed, pre-set criteria. In addition, the return on investment of AI tools for use in stroke clinical trials must be considered. Further analyses are needed to determine if the significant investments in computing power for Gen AI models to develop, implement, and maintain AI tools are worthwhile to the clinical trial enterprise through increased recruitment and other trial efficiencies that may reduce overall trial costs.

The workshop participants were queried about one thing that AI could do to make clinical and clinical trial workflow better. Responses included AI models that can extract information from non-contrast CT equivalent to that from CT perfusion imaging (at-risk brain), characterization of the clot for interventionalists, tapping into the EHR for data extraction and alerts for acute trial enrollments, rapid access to surrogates using available public databases for informed consent, decision support for the enrollment and consent process for coordinators and investigators, AI rewriting informed consents to improve readability particularly for those with lower reading levels, real-time translation of the informed consent into other languages, and AI assistance with IRB applications. AI’s future potential uses are likely limited only by the creativity of those who design the programs, and even AI programs themselves may help to identify future applications. Regardless, the quality and understanding of the data used to train the AI, the human element needed to ensure training is successful, and the clinician and trialist at the bedside - the “humans in the loop” - will be necessary to maximize AI’s effectiveness in clinical practice and clinical trials (Figure 1). Moreover, expansion of this technology to low- and middle-income countries to enhance thrombolytic and endovascular therapy decision-making, including fine-tuning of models with data from local populations, is an important future task.

### Platform and Other Innovations in Clinical Trial Methodology

Clinical trials of treatments for stroke have generally utilized 2-arm, and sometime 3-arm,18,19 randomized designs to evaluate interventions against a control arm. Prior trial designs have also included a factorial design where two questions are answered in a single trial.20 However, this one-step and one-question-at-a-time approach is slow, expensive, and limits advances in the field. In addition, building the infrastructure for a single clinical trial is time-consuming and wasteful when the infrastructure is dismantled at the end of a trial.

The evolution of nationally funded stroke networks is a major advance to help facilitate quick uptake of approved and funded trials,21 but still doesn’t address the efficiency of answering more than one clinical question at a time. Platform trials—randomized clinical trials designed to evaluate multiple interventions that may enter and exit the ongoing platform based on a master protocol—accelerate the investigation of multiple therapeutic options within a single infrastructure.22

Platform trials began nearly 20 years old, but they are brand new in the field of stroke. Platform trials were pioneered first in cancer with STAMPEDE (prostate cancer),23 I-SPY (breast cancer).24 and GBM AGILE (glioblastoma).25 Neurologic platform trials followed with Healey ALS (amyotrophic lateral sclerosis) in 202026 and the EPPIC NET or HEAL (pain),27 but the first platform trials for stroke (STEP - StrokeNet Thrombectomy Endovascular Platform) and ACT-GLOBAL - A Multi-Factorial, Multi-Arm, Multi-Stage, Randomised, Global Adaptive Platform Trial for Stroke)22 didn’t start until 2024.

Platform trials usually require a master protocol to accelerate the investigation of multiple therapeutic options within a single infrastructure. This in turn increases time- and cost-efficiencies in the development of interventions for patients with strokes that can save lives and improve outcomes. In the context of acute ischemic stroke, two new platform trials have been established, the STEP trial and ACT-GLOBAL to address multiple therapeutic questions simultaneously using a multifactorial design including Bayesian modeling and other adaptive features. Recruitment into the STEP Platform and AcT GLOBAL has started. These trials are designed to maximize the information obtained from each participant, to align clinical research more closely with the complexities of clinical care, and to accelerate the identification of effective therapies.

Platform trials can address multiple scientific questions in an area of medicine at the same time within the same trial structure. One key feature of platform trials are shared controls across multiple interventions. Platform trials often have a single unifying master trial protocol. The master protocol outlines overarching aspects of the platform trial such as description of the overall population to be studied on the platform (for example acute ischemic strokes with a visible vessel occlusion for STEP), research procedures that are applicable across all patients enrolled in the trial, ideally a single primary outcome, and the statistical model to make decisions regarding interventions or comparators. It also outlines general rules around decision making in the platform such as interim analysis frequency and the types of decisions to be made (superiority, inferiority, futility, equivalence, etc.).

Once the master protocol is defined, specific questions are then added to this platform as domains. Domains are studies of mutually exclusive interventions or strategies. Domain Specific Appendices to the Master Protocol outline aspects specific to those questions. For example, the STEP platform’s first domain, Domain A, evaluates the efficacy of endovascular thrombectomy (EVT) compared to medical management (MM) for two distinct patient populations: 1- those with a large vessel occlusion and mild presenting deficits and 2- those with medium/distal vessel occlusion. Decisions regarding superiority or inferiority of EVT compared to MM are made for these two distinct populations (called population strata) separately. As the platform progresses, additional testing of additional interventions such cytoprotective drugs, background medical therapy or rescue intraarterial therapy etc., can be added. Participants can participate in answering all questions for which they are eligible (if the questions allow co-enrollment). A hypothetical example of the Platform approach is provided in Figure 2.

Many innovative features can be added in the analysis plan of these specific trial questions such as adaptive randomization, borrowing of information from adjacent patient groups, and shared controls. Platform trials can be enduring or circumscribed in terms of duration depending on the availability of scientific questions to ask and funding. Cost efficiency of the platforms come from contractual and regulatory efficiencies related to one single Master Protocol, allowing a single participant to answer multiple questions (muti-factorial nature), shared controls, and other innovative features. Another advantage of platform-driven studies is that they can decrease competition among trials for enrollment within a similar or overlapping patient populations.

While platform trials increase efficiency and potentially decrease the time needed to answer important clinical scientific questions within the field of stroke, they also introduce complexity to standard workflow during execution of the trial procedures at the enrolling site, particularly during the process of informed consent. For the STEP platform, prior stroke patients who are part of our community engagement core for StrokeNet provided feedback about a single consent with check boxes for potential participants to fill out what types of trials that they would choose to participate in. They greatly disliked this approach and much preferred individual consent for specific questions that are only presented as they become eligible along the spectrum of care. For example, a patient may decide whether to enroll in a trial of a new cytoprotective in the first minutes and hours after stroke onset and 24 hours later decide to participate in another part of the platform trial testing an anti-edema medication. In the acute setting, patients and their families are already stressed by the emotional impact of having a stroke. Deciding whether to participate in a clinical trial testing a new therapy adds to their stress and consideration of multiple types of therapies at the same time may be overwhelming. A one-page patient information sheet summarizing the overall concept of what a platform trial is a helpful aid at the bedside for patients and their families. A larger endeavor would be to increase clinical trial literacy through education of the public and populations at risk for stroke about the potential advantages of platform trials for improving quality of care for stroke patients. Initiatives to do this could be undertaken by NIH or organizations like the American Heart Association.

Other challenges of platform trials include the use of non-concurrent controls that can lead to potential biases if the standard-of-care and patient outcomes change over time and which require more complex statistical approaches. Addressing data-sharing and intellectual property when multiple industry partners are part of a platform trial is another challenge.

While to this point, stroke platform trials have focused on acute treatment of ischemic stroke, platform trial designs for acute treatment of intracerebral and subarachnoid hemorrhage, secondary stroke prevention, and stroke rehabilitation may be used in the future.

### Pragmatic Trial Design

Future acute ischemic stroke clinical trials should also incorporate elements of pragmatic trial design when possible. There is a continuum of pragmatism in clinical trial designs ranging from highly selective (explanatory) to real world clinical practice (pragmatic).28,29 Clinical trials investigating the efficacy of new, unapproved drugs, procedures, or devices are inherently explanatory, meaning they aim to support or not support a beneficial effect of the treatment under idealized conditions. Examples of explanatory stroke clinical trials abound, including the landmark trials showing efficacy of endovascular therapy for select patients with large vessel occlusion and acute ischemic stroke30–32 and the many previous trials testing novel cytoprotectant agents. 33,34 Importantly, explanatory trials can still have elements of pragmatic design. These may include executing the trial in clinical settings where the therapy may be implemented in clinical practice (for example, community-based or non-academic hospitals, if applicable), broadening the population in which the treatment is being tested as deemed appropriate, and using endpoints relevant to patients and their families to complement mechanistic endpoints.

On the other end of the continuum of pragmatic trials are ones that aim to assess the effectiveness of treatments when they are implemented into routine clinical care rather than under idealized conditions.35 These trials often address simple clinical questions such as comparing two existing treatment strategies to see if one is better than the other. 36 Examples include the Early versus Late Initiation of Direct Oral Anticoagulants in Post-ischemic Stroke Patients with Atrial Fibrillation (ELAN) trial 36 and the Optimal timing of anticoagulation after acute ischemic stroke with atrial fibrillation (OPTIMAS) trial37 comparing early versus later initiation of direct oral anticoagulants after acute ischemic stroke in patients with atrial fibrillation. By comparing existing treatments, embedding trials procedures into normal clinical workflows, and using data from the electronic health record, the costs associated with these types of pragmatic trials can be lower and the infrastructure less complex. Pragmatic designs hopefully increase the chances that a trial is accomplished successfully, timely, and inexpensively. These more pragmatic trials were recognized by the workshop participants as important to complement purely explanatory trials. Ideally, trials that merge the features of platform trials (more than one question at a time) with a spectrum of pragmatic approaches are most likely to quickly advance the overall field and treatment options for patients.

Finally, the stroke research community needs more community and patient engagement. This should include input from the boots-on-the-ground medical personnel (EMTs, physicians at transferring and receiving facilities, and study coordinators) who enroll and treat stroke patients in clinical trials. Common goals for a trial should be established to minimize patient and investigator burden in trial participation, extend trial participation to community-based settings whenever possible, and quickly disseminate trial results to patients, clinicians, and the public.

### Summary

Artificial intelligence models and platform, pragmatic and patient-centered trial designs are important new tools to answer clinical questions that are important to patients and the clinicians who care for them. Like any tool, understanding how they work, their best uses, and their limitations are critical for accelerating successful new treatments for stroke.
